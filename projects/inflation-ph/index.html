Bangko Sentral set to hike rates for the first time since 2018

Inflation wave spreading across the globe prompts central bank action in the Philippines


May 19, 2022


After four years, the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas, the Philippine central bank, appears poised to increase policy rates when it meets on Thursday, May 19.
Currently, the overnight borrowing rate stands at 2%, a record-low set at the height of the pandemic in 2020 to ensure there is adequate liquidity in the financial system to support the economy while lockdowns prevented businesses to operate, and consumers to go out and spend.
While the pandemic is still within our midst, the economy has already recovered, and gross domestic product is back at its pre-pandemic levels. Consequently, the challenge for the central bank has shifted back to its old nemesis: soaring inflation.
Consumer prices, as measured by the consumer price index, breached past the 2-4% annual target anew in April to hit 4.9%. The four-month inflation stood at 4.1%, no thanks to supply constraints from food to oil that continued to push prices up while consumer demand is still on the rise from pandemic slowdown.

This was how inflation and BSP's benchmark rates tread over the past four years.

In 2018, inflationpeaked at 6.9% in October after a shortage in rice supplies pushed prices up.

The central bank acted quickly, that even before things got worse, it raised it started hiking its benchmark rate.

By the following year, year-over-year inflation steadily stabilized, partly because of high prices the previous year.

After a series of hikes, BSP started bringing down the borrowing rates in Q2 also in support of slowing economic growth that slumped to 5.4%, the slowest since Q1 2015.

And then the pandemic happened. At the time, BSP started cutting rates as early as February 2020.

From 4% at the start of the year, rates dropped to 2% by November 2020.

Inflation was not a problem at the time because demand was subdued due to lockdowns.

That has been the case until the economy started to reopen and eased restrictions. Inflation briefly breached the 4% upper-end target of the central bank.

BSP downplayed the target breach, saying it was "transitory"because the economy was gaining ground from years of lockdown.

Indeed, inflation went back to 3.7% by June 2021 and dropped further to 3% by January 2022.

But Ukraine was invaded in February, and with it came an oil price surge that saw inflation shot back up to 4.9% in April.

The central bank, before May 19, did not act to respond to rising commodity costs and kept rates at record-lows.

That meant inflation was hovering more than double the benchmark rate. Put simply, inflation is eating up the interest charged on money.
