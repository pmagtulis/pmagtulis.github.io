Stagflation risks? GDP slumps as inflation pops in the US

Q1 GDP surprises on the downside, raising concern the world's largest economy is losing steam


May 1, 2022


After the US plunged into its first contraction since the pandemic in the first quarter, is the world's largest economy at risk of falling into a stagflation? Economists said risks are rising, but it is too early to tell.
The dreaded combo of soaring inflation, recession and lower unemployment was fanned anew by a Thursday report that showed that gross domestic product (GDP) slumped 1.4% year-on-year in the first three months.
On its own, the data did not look worrisome, especially when you consider that GDP was coming off from an unusually high base last year and therefore distorting the data to the downside. But that the economy appears to be losing steam while prices are rising and employment, while still at healthy levels, is showing signs of weakness, has some observers raise red flags.
"The announced downturn of GDPâ€¦is certainly a possible sign that we are entering a period of stagflation," Jeffrey Sachs, economics professor at Columbia University School of International and Public Affairs, said.
"It's too early to say, but it's very possible," he added.
Others are more optimistic. "Well, that (report) will get all the stagflation worries out in force! However, we wouldn't get too carried away since the details are not as bad as the headline figure suggests," James Knightley, chief international economist at ING, said in a research note.
The concern though is not unfounded. In 1973, when oil-producing economies in the Middle East imposed an oil embargo against the US and Europe, petroleum prices shot up and as did costs of other commodities dependent on oil as raw material. That, in turn, hurt businesses which were forced to lay off people, resulting into massive joblessness, and a recession a year after.

This time around, oil is again the culprit, no thanks to Russia's invasion of Ukraine that prompted the West to impose sanctions on it and Kremlin to retaliate by restricting oil supplies. Lingering supply shocks as a result of manufacturing shutdown in China are also not helping, at a time consumer demand is roaring back from pandemic disruptions.
However, while conditions that account for stagflation are set in stone, economists often find it difficult to agree when and how it happens. One question is the timing. For Sachs, stagflation happens when the situations it is associated with last for "generally two quarters or six months."
Of GDP, employment, and inflation, so far only consumer prices have consistently trended upwards since February last year, breaching the Federal Reserve's annual target of 2%.

It is normal for inflation to go up or down

But during crises, price swings tend to be larger or smaller than usual.

In June 2002, inflation hit a low of 1.1%, over a year after the 9/11 attacks crippled the economy.

The global financial crisis handed the economy a heavier damage though, resulting in a deflation in 2009 which means prices were instead getting lower.

The pandemic had a slightly similar effect of depressing prices due to economic inactivity.

But when the economy reopened, inflation came back with a vengeance. From about 2% in March 2021..

...it accelerated to 8.5% in March 2022.

In direct contrast, unemployment has fallen from its peak of 14.7% at the height of the pandemic in March to just 3.6% two years after.
Another contention is whether the weakness in the economy represents a more structural phenomenon that tends to linger longer. This may not be the case, according to Carl Weinberg, chief economist at High Frequency Economics, who said last September that spiking consumer prices is a "temporary" impact of higher oil prices because of the Ukraine war.
Jeffrey Frankel, economics professor at Harvard Kennedy School, agreed, pointing out that much of the "negative surprise" on the latest GDP number "was made up entirely of a rise in imports." "The Ukraine crisis is a factor in recent increases in energy prices and food pricesâ€¦But effects have not shown up in GDP or employment statistics as of now," he said.

Like inflation, US GDP has had its highs and lows.

The oil crisis, for instance, pushed GDP down 8% on an annual basis in the second quarter of 1980.

The financial crisis, meanwhile, prompted a recession , which is defined as two successive quarters of contraction.

At the peak of the pandemic, the economy also shrank by most since 1960.

GDP plummeted by a whopping 31.2% year-on-year at the height of lockdowns in the second quarter of 2020.

But unlike 2009, the economy did not suffer a recession and quickly bounced back.

All eyes are now on the second quarter data.
