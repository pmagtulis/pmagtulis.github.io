But there's a story behind it.

This chart tracks additional minutes spent by commuters during peak hours inside New York City's trains.

On average, commuters are now spending an additional 26 seconds on board trains than usual. That's the the longest since March 2020.

In some train lines, travel tends to be longer than average like in B-line

While in some, travel time like in the 6-line has significantly improved.

In total, riding 11 out of the 25 train lines would mean additional time spent onboard toward your destination as of February.

That some trains are running slower should not come as a surprise. Last August, Robert Paaswell, a civil engineering professor at City College of New York, said the reprieve of faster trains at the time was not meant to last. “Since ridership was down, the trains could move faster. Plain and simple,” he said then.
“Ridership, at least in the subway, tracks downtown workers,” Paaswell said. “Once more people ride the trains, the trains are gonna slow down.” he said at the time.
Indeed, that appears to be already happening. After the spread of Omicron variant blunted office return, companies are finally ordering workers to return. By March 30, office occupancy has ticked back up to 36% in New York City, data from Kastle Systems, a property management firm, showed.
Not only are travel time getting longer in trains, but waiting times in platforms are also getting longer. Data from the Subway Dashboard maintained by the Metropolitan Transit Authority (MTA) showed that passengers are spending an average of 1.19 minutes than they usually do during peak hours to wait for their ride. In short, not only is the time to reach your destination getting longer, but just hitching the next train has gone a lot more tedious.

The good news is wait hours are still not as bad as their pre-pandemic levels, although they are slowly getting there. That is an impending problem with no easy fix as the subway continues to grapple with budget problems, which in turn, limits the government's ability to invest and modernize the aging rail system.
In November, New York scored an additional $3.6 billion from President Biden's infrastructure bill to funnel to intercity rails. The MTA, which operates the train system, is expected to get $10 billion from the measure.
Apart from federal subsidies, the MTA has been trying to raise funds on its own for the train system. One good thing that may come out of a potential increase in number of passengers will be more fare collections. Fares make up 38% of the MTA's annual budget, data showed.
