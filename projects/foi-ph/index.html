Duterte's freedom of information is not so free

FOI improved information access. But gov't has also kept sensitive ones under wraps


April 17, 2022

When Philippine President Rodrigo Duterte signed an executive order on Freedom for Information (FOI) shortly after he was elected in 2016, his spokesperson, Martin Andanar, said that “contracts involving the use of public funds will be made available” to the public.
But nearly six years after, not only are contents of most government contracts still inaccessible, but previously available documents like annual filings that reveal public officials' source of wealth have likewise become difficult to obtain, even for members of the media.
Such is the irony of Duterte's FOI order, supposedly an answer to decades of public clamor for government transparency. The presidential order was considered an easier route than legislation, and Duterte was applauded for fulfilling a campaign promise that his predecessors failed to accomplish. However, an analysis of the inquiries lodged through the FOI website showed very few requests are being granted, of which many are "low-ball" requests, as what one scholar said.
Since November 2016, less than half of over 110,000 FOI requests filed were fulfilled, our analysis showed. That is way below the 80% approval rate the Presidential Communications Office, which oversees the FOI program, has set for agencies under the Executive branch covered by Duterte's order. About one in four requests was denied, while the rest were at varying levels of clearance as of April 10.

Worse, headline numbers do not tell the whole story.
Sensitive information like government contracts is kept under wraps. Part of the problem is the severe lack of state resources for FOI and citizens' unfamiliarity with FOI. But much are also traced from laws that overrule an executive order. When this happens, public offices easily get away in denying a request, leaving the public at the losing end.
“Indeed, by all indications, Duterte sticks out as the lie of his own Freedom of Information edict,” said the Philippine Center for Investigative Journalism (PCIJ), which got repeated denials in requests for Duterte's Statements of Assets, Liabilities and Net Worth (SALN).

The number of FOI requests getting denied have been on a declining trend.

But looking closely, some of the most important requests that seek government accountability are getting denied. This includes the Statements of Assets Liabilities and Net Worth (SALN).

By law, public officers are required to make their SALN accessible to the public.

But in the past five years, the number of FOI denials for SALN far outpaced that of average.

In 2018 alone, nearly all of SALN requests filed through the FOI portal were denied, supposedly because most of them were filed with the wrong agency.

While the number of FOI requests being denied has declined, there were still over one in five requests for SALN that did not get granted.

Writer's block
For local journalists, this is simply the Duterte administration trying to escape accountability. Apart from SALN, which in the past was used to prove corruption accusations against a former president and a chief justice, requests for information about the controversial war on drugs through FOI had been blocked. Our analysis showed eight requests about war on drugs, three of which were denied and four were granted.
Another one was deemed “accepted”, meaning the agency that received the request ruled that it was a valid request and something it can supply. The next step should have been for the agency to provide the data, but that did not happen. The request was made in June 2021 and technically remains unaddressed to date. Under FOI rules, an agency has 15 days to respond about data that are readily available, and up to 35 days for those that need digging.
One FOI request about war on drugs came from Lian Nami Buan, a reporter covering the justice beat for Rappler, a local news site. In February 2021, she requested access to the justice department's preliminary report on the drug war, a result of a state-led review triggered by rising cases of human rights violations in the program. The department issued press statements about the report but did not release its entirety. Buan's request was denied, with no reason given.
"FOI, as a system, was never really helpful in my experience,” Buan said. “I use it a lot, but of the successful requests, the data given me was not the data I asked for… So in a way, all these denials were expected," she said.

It's not only journalists who face hurdles in securing information. In one case, a researcher asked the environment department for a copy of the impact assessment of the proposed Pasig River Expressway in Metro Manila, a privately-funded project opposed by environmentalists over risks of massive environmental damage. After just four days, the agency denied the request.
Paul Jason Perez, an assistant professor at University of the Philippines who has researched on FOI, said the current FOI mechanism is far from ideal. Our analysis showed that the bulk of requests were tagged for “research” purposes and majority contained the word “data” in them, but Perez said most of these requests should no longer have been made in the first place.
“These things are low-ball requests that should have been in government websites already, or at least most of them, and should not have been the subject of FOI,” he said.
“When you weave through the noise, requests which the FOI is for, are in fact, being rejected.”
Indeed, in other countries like the US and Brazil, FOI is used to uncover wrongdoing in government, and seek accountability from erring public officials. But not in the Philippines, where one problem, Perez said, is that freedom of information is not a law, and hence, can easily be circumvented by public officials by citing legislation.
That is most often the case on tax records. In her most recent FOI request, Buan wanted the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) to release a copy of its demand letter to former senator Ferdinand "Bongbong" Marcos Jr., who is running for president and is being accused of tax evasion. The BIR, in its response, cited confidentiality provided under the Tax Code as well as data privacy laws to deny the request. The same reply was received by ABS-CBN and GMA News.

In December 2016, Robert Anthony Siy III, who works for the Pasig City local government, asked the transport department for a copy of the Metro Manila Road Transit Rationalization Study, which was meant to improve the capital region's traffic situation. As a transport economist, he would have wanted to assess the study, and possibly give suggestions.
It took the agency six months to process the request, but eventually it was approved. "Your FOI request is approved. I enclose a copy of the information you requested," the reply said, but the attachment was nowhere to be found. "I no longer followed it up with them since I already got the document from other means," Siy said.
Kris Ablan, deputy presidential spokesperson whose office oversees the FOI portal, said there is not so much he can do to fix these problems. "When it comes to how individual agencies handle their requests, it's up to them. We constantly remind them, but some are just really strict about information they cannot share," Ablan said.
Lack of resources
The problem with FOI can likewise be traced from dismal resources allocated to the program. A clear example of this is the employment of so-called “FOI receiving officers,” which in an ideal world, would have been designated the sole job of handling FOI queries, but in reality, are rank-and-file employees with other duties but are forced to take on the task of screening FOI requests.
"I grew white hair just answering them one-by-one," a 28-year-old former FOI officer, who declined to be named because she no longer works in the agency, said in jest.
The job is no joke. You read and draft a reply for each request. Typically, a response template is provided so replies can immediately be sent out. But the actual work for an FOI receiving officer happens in monitoring and following up with offices where actual data requested would come from. The FOI receiving officer would have to update the portal from time to time so that the person who filed the request is informed of the status of their request.
The whole thing becomes tedious if there is an unusual number of requests such as the experience of some agencies like the Department of Social Welfare and Development (DSWD) at the height of pandemic. At the time, DSWD was swamped with queries from citizens asking where and how to get their pandemic cash aid, which the agency distributed. Unfortunately, none of those qualified as FOI requests, and thus were denied.

Hence, the other problem: citizens are not that informed yet as to the proper use of FOI. To address this gap, Ablan said PCO is conducting FOI education caravans. Policies are also introduced, such as the “no wrong-door policy” enforced two years ago, which mandates agencies to reroute requests filed with the wrong office, instead of denying them altogether. Data show over one in four denied requests were because they were submitted to the wrong office.
If anything, the situation is not unique to the Philippines and Gregory Michener, an FOI scholar from Brazil, said these challenges can be traced from the relatively young FOI regime in the country.
"This is in contrast with countries like the US where you will see agencies bend over backwards to meet your needs," Michener said.
"What is important in the process is that officials, in good faith, try their best to answer or redirect citizens towards the right service," he said. Prinz Magtulis
